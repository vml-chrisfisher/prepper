Weekly Newsletter No. 18
Hi all,

I hope this Wednesday finds you taking a needed breath. It’s enthralling being in the middle of the holiday. The anticipation of family and friends. Planning of menu and the prep that comes along with it. Along with the proper way to endcap the year to friends near and far. And now it’s over. I look forward to this moment. It gives me time and the excuse to reset things in the kitchen. I tend to clean out the fridge. Resetting all of the open homemade pickling and jams with fresh jars. Taking stock of all of the many pieces of Parmesan I’ve accumulated over the months. Though it also gives me the excuse to take inventory of all my kitchen utensils. A good purge in the kitchen feels like it gives the house a good release off its shoulder. Which just feels needed after the holidays and the emotions that come with this time of year. This last year took me through spaces that needed to be confronted and allowed a solid base to be formed. It was finding who I was and opening up to that and releasing that partially through Knife and Fish. I can’t express my appreciation enough that you decided to jump on this train and have stayed with me. Next year feels like it will be a good space to move into and I can’t wait. With that let’s get to it for the last time this year.
Blackcreek Mercantile & Trading Co.
Using
The luster on all of my walnut spoons and spatulas have needed a good oiling for a time. I’ve used walnut oil in the past though Blackcreek Mercantile & Trading Co. has their cutting board oil that is more neutral. A small company that is all about small goods for the kitchen, most notably wooden spoons, cutting boards, and their proprietary blend of cutting board and knife oils.
Weck Canning Jars
Using
Ball Canning jars have been the nostalgic canning jar though the rusting lids make them seem a bit off-putting. Weck jars with their rubber seals have become my new standard and I haven't looked back.
Step Inside Some of the World's Most Historic Hotels
Reading
Step Inside Some of the World's Most Historic Hotels. I’ve always been enthralled by architecture and find that inns and hotels offer an excuse to be extravagant but also remain grounded in reality as they are public spaces with many people passing through every day. The architecture of a good hotel must not only be versatile—meeting the demands of conferences, diplomats, weddings, and weary travelers all in one—but also timeless, a feat which is hard to achieve and is endlessly impressive. Architectural Digest is one of my guilty pleasures, and although there’s minimal reading to be done, this article touches on some of what makes America’s historic hotels so special.

That’s it for this week and the year. Enjoy the rest of the year and be safe out there.
