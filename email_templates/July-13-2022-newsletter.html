Weekly Newsletter No. 40
Hello All,

Two weeks in France and the Independence Day holiday adds up - it’s been a bit. Many of you have sent messages asking how France was. To keep it short, it was absolutely everything we wanted it to be. The saying “We did a lot of things and a lot of nothing” is an excellent summary. We both learned that a two week vacation is a more desirable timeframe. It allows you to thoroughly enjoy your time. You feel less inclined to quickly seek out all of the greatest hits and more inclined to soak it all in. The seafood was abundant and so was the wine, specifically red, lots of conversation, a bit of adventure, and lots and lots of cards.

Leaving was hard, but it gave me an appreciation of how the French have figured out the good parts of life. Things move slower. There was an overall sense of appreciating what they’ve perfected. It made me take pause at the basics of cooking - bread, butter, cheese, and just the simplicity of the Jambon-Beurre.

With summer in full swing, it was nice to come home to see the okra plants blooming and fruit starting to appear. I do regret not going full steam with a large garden, but I decided to make this year more of an experiment with my herbs and okra specifically. Regenerative farming has become an interest, and so thinking through those thoughts on a smaller scale is on the drawing board as well.

With this longer than normal lead-in, let’s get to it.

Chef’s Fable by Eater
Reading
If there was inspiration toward regenerative farming, it would have been the story behind Chef Dan Barber and Blue Hill at Stone Barns. His vision represented what the Farm to Table movement should be. Cooking with solely what came from the farm. Creating a farm whose ecosystem was enriched by the care of its animals, which then trickled down to rich soil, which then helped create rich fruits and veggies, and ultimately a rich food source for the restaurant and its animals. An aspiration that one would think was all too good or one that hadn’t shown its backside. Eater found that backside and the article “Chef’s Fable” shows an operation that lost its way toward its vision. I can’t fault one for things mentioned in the article. The culture we live in sets such a high standard to be the new standard. Though forgiveness for mistakes is rarely taken.
