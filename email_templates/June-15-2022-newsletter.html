Another Happy Wednesday to everyone. If you are part of the Midwest right now you know of the heat wave we are experiencing right now. High temps and almost a consistent 100% humidity make for the true dog days of summer. However, that hasn’t stopped me from using the smoker, my father-in-law recently gifted me. It’s a Kamadodo Joe, cousin of the Green Egg, and the learning to dial it in seems to be a steeper learning curve. Though I’m all in. I’ve been trying to hone in a meal course we had on our recent trip to Charleston which was a simple jar of smoked oysters and saltines with a Plymouth gin martini. Both Gin and oysters fascinate me. They have deep geological heritage and their bespoke taste is so driven by it. Just in the US, the amount of different ones just on one coast seems to be endless but every one of them has a different brine, size, and feel to them. Sucking one down from the shell always pulls a little grit from the shell. One of the locations we are staying in in France is known for its oysters. On most trips, I’m usually ready to leave, when the time comes, though this goes around may be different. With that, let’s get to it.
Smoked Oysters
Cooking
Smoked oysters are a true treat. While there is definitely a camp that can’t get past the texture of a raw oyster, a smoked oyster is firmer and has allowed its brine to smoke back into the oyster. So add smoke and concentrate the oyster’s brine back into itself and it’s truly something to enjoy. Preserving them in olive oil will allow them to keep longer. I enjoy them simply on top of a saltine cracker, and a dab of hot sauce.
Plymouth Martini
Drinking
There are several types of gin though Plymouth gin is one of the few types of gin that is geologically guarded as well. Plymouth Gin can only be made in Plymouth, England. Funny enough, right now there is only one distillery there called the Plymouth Gin Co. Plymouth Gin tends to be a cleaner, drier gin and translates straight to a Plymouth Gin Martini.
“When You’re a Guitar Player, You Can Really Hear How the Differences Matter”: Jack White Talks Creativity and Gear
Reading
If you don’t understand the genius of Jack White, his music and pure talent with the guitar then read no more. Though if you have any affinity for Jack White, what he has done for music, its preservation and the guitar as an instrument then “When You’re a Guitar Player, You Can Really Hear How the Differences Matter” will draw you. Speaking about how much the details matter and how those details translate to sound in a way that only Jack White can explain is fascinating. It shows how craftsmanship goes beyond just one technique but about studying the craft as a whole is what makes the cream float to the top.
Entering Heaven Alive by Jack White
Listening
I can’t say that I’ve been a true lover of Jack White’s music. But I have always had a respect for what Jack White represents in music. His talent goes beyond what could be listed as one of the Top 10 guitarists of all time. Bringing back the pressing of records along with the details of how instruments should be built. It’s truly impressive. Entering Heaven Alive is Jack White’s latest album has a full release date in July. The early release tracks tend to represent something a bit more melancholy than what one would be used to from a Jack White album. Intrigued is what represents me right now.
